# Military Retirement Planning in 2026: Legacy vs BRS, TSP, and What to Know Before You Leave

Military retirement planning is one of the biggest financial decisions many service members will make, but it is also one of the most misunderstood. The rules depend on when a person joined, which retirement system applies to them, and how they plan to combine pension income, TSP savings, and civilian income later on.

A lot of service members wait too long to learn the basics of retirement planning. They may assume their pension will be simple, or they may not realize how much TSP contributions can matter over time. That can lead to missed opportunities, especially for people who are deciding whether to stay in longer or begin preparing for a transition.

## Legacy vs BRS
The first thing many people want to understand is which retirement system applies to them. Service members who joined before January 1, 2018 are usually in the Legacy system, while those who joined on or after that date are usually in the Blended Retirement System, or BRS.

The main difference is that Legacy is a defined benefit plan with a stronger pension formula at 20 years, while BRS combines a smaller pension with the option to build savings through TSP. For some people, that can create a very different long-term outcome.

## Why TSP matters under BRS
TSP planning becomes especially important under BRS because the system includes an agency match for eligible contributions. That match is one of the most valuable benefits available to service members who want to build retirement savings while still serving.

In practical terms, contributing enough to get the full match can make a huge difference over time. It is one of the easiest ways to increase retirement wealth without needing a complicated strategy from day one.

## What a retirement calculator can show
A military retirement calculator is useful because it gives people a simple estimate of what retirement income might look like. It can help estimate monthly pension income, compare Legacy and BRS outcomes, and show how service years and rank can change the final result.

That kind of estimate is especially helpful when a service member is evaluating whether to stay in longer, start saving more aggressively, or explore the impact of a lump sum option under BRS.

## The importance of planning early
Retirement planning should not start only when someone is close to leaving service. The earlier a service member understands the system, the easier it becomes to make decisions about TSP contributions, future income, and life after retirement.

For many people, the biggest mistake is assuming that the pension alone will be enough. In reality, a strong retirement plan often includes more than one source of income, including pension, TSP, VA benefits, and civilian savings.

## Common questions service members ask
Some of the most common retirement questions include:

- Which system do I belong to?
- How does my pension change if I stay past 20 years?
- How much does TSP contribute to my long-term plan?
- What happens if I choose a lump sum option?
- Will my retirement pay increase over time?

These questions matter because they shape how a service member approaches the final years of service and the first years after transition.

## Final thoughts
Military retirement planning works best when it starts with a clear understanding of the system and a realistic estimate of future income. A retirement calculator can provide a useful starting point, but the bigger benefit comes from understanding how pension, TSP, and long-term savings fit together.

If you are planning your military transition, start with a simple estimate first. Then use that estimate to build a more complete approach for retirement, savings, and future financial stability.
