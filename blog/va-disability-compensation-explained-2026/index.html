VA Disability Compensation Explained: What Veterans Should Know in 2026

Practical guide · Updated 2026

VA disability compensation is one of the most important benefits available to veterans, but it is also one of the most misunderstood. Many veterans want to understand how the system works, what it pays, and whether they should file a claim or appeal an existing decision.

The good news is that the basics are not as complicated as they first appear. At a high level, VA disability compensation is tax-free financial support for veterans with service-connected conditions. If a condition is linked to military service, the VA may assign a percentage-based disability rating, which can affect the amount of monthly compensation a veteran receives.

How VA ratings work

One reason the topic feels confusing is that the VA does not simply add every condition together as a straight number. Instead, it uses a combined ratings system that considers how each condition affects a veteran’s overall functioning.

Common conditions that lead to claims

Some of the most common conditions connected to VA disability claims include PTSD, tinnitus, hearing loss, migraines, back pain, knee injuries, sleep apnea, and anxiety or depression. These conditions are common because they can stem from deployments, training, injuries, or long-term stress during service.

Why a calculator can help

A VA disability calculator is not an official VA decision tool, but it can be a practical planning resource. It can help veterans estimate what a combined rating might look like and how different disability percentages could affect monthly compensation.

What veterans should keep in mind

If you are considering filing a claim, the most important step is to collect strong documentation. That may include service records, medical records, private physician statements, and a clear explanation of how the condition affects work, sleep, relationships, and daily functioning.

Final thoughts

VA disability compensation is a major benefit, but the process can be difficult to navigate without context. The best approach is to start with a simple overview, use a calculator for planning, and gather the strongest evidence possible before moving forward.
