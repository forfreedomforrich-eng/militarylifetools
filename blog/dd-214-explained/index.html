DD-214 Explained Step by Step: Every Field on Your Military Separation Document

July 10, 2026 | 12 min read | By Military Life Tools

If you're separating from the military—or just got your DD-214 in the mail—you've probably noticed it's a thick, multi-page document packed with technical fields, codes, and acronyms. The DD-214 (Certificate of Release or Discharge on Active Duty) is arguably the most important document a service member will ever receive. It determines your eligibility for VA benefits, GI Bill education assistance, home loans, and even civilian employment opportunities.

This guide breaks down every major section of the DD-214, explains what each field means, and tells you exactly how to use your document to claim benefits. Bookmark this page—it's your reference for life after service.

What is a DD-214 and Why Does It Matter?

The DD-214 is issued by the Department of Defense when a service member completes active duty, is released from active duty, or is separated from the military. It serves as official proof of military service and contains critical information about your character of service, periods of service, awards, decorations, and eligibility for veterans benefits.

Without a DD-214, you cannot:

  • Apply for VA disability compensation
  • Enroll in the Post-9/11 GI Bill
  • Qualify for a VA home loan
  • Access Veterans Health Administration (VHA) benefits
  • Apply for many federal and state veterans programs
  • Prove military service to employers (which often offers hiring preference)

⚠️ Critical: Keep Your DD-214 Safe

Store your original DD-214 in a fireproof safe or safety deposit box. Make certified copies for benefit applications. If you lose it, you can request a replacement from the National Archives—but that process takes 10-30 business days.

Field-by-Field Breakdown of the DD-214

Block 1: Name and Social Security Number

This block contains your full legal name (as it appears in military records) and your Social Security Number. Double-check for accuracy—errors here can delay benefit processing. If your name has changed (marriage, legal change), note it for future correspondence.

Block 2: Branch of Service

Indicates which military branch you served in: Army, Navy, Air Force, Marine Corps, Coast Guard, or Space Force. This determines which service's regulations apply to your benefits.

Block 3: Reserve/National Guard Component

Shows whether you served on active duty, in the reserves, or in the National Guard. This is critical for determining eligibility for certain benefits like the Reserve GI Bill versus the Post-9/11 GI Bill.

Block 4: Dates of Service

Lists your start and end dates of active duty. These dates determine your length of service, which directly impacts:

  • GI Bill eligibility: Minimum 90 days aggregate service after Sept. 10, 2001
  • VA disability: Establishes the period during which service-connected conditions must have occurred
  • Retirement: Determines if you qualify for military retirement (20+ years)

Block 5: Character of Service

This is the most important block for benefits eligibility. It indicates whether your service was honorable, under honorable conditions, general, or under other-than-honorable conditions. For VA benefits, you generally need an honorable or general under honorable conditions characterization.

Block 6: Discharge or Release Type

Specifies the type of separation: Entry-Level Separation, Administrative, Honorable, General, Under Other Than Honorable Conditions, Bad Conduct (by Board), Dishonorable (by Court-Martial), or Discharge for Convenience of Government.

Block 7: Awards and Recommendations

Lists medals, ribbons, badges, and citations received during service. While not always required for basic benefits, these decorations can:

  • Qualify you for Purple Heart VA disability exemptions
  • Provide preference in federal hiring
  • Enhance your resume for civilian employment

Block 8: Periods of Active Service Outside the U.S.

Details any overseas deployments or tours. This is relevant for combat-related benefits, hostile fire pay, and certain VA disability presumptions (e.g., Agent Orange exposure in Vietnam).

Block 9: MOS/AFSC/SOC/Rating

Your Military Occupational Specialty (Army/Marine Corps), Air Force Specialty Code (Air Force), Special Operations Code (Special Forces), or Navy Rating. This determines which civilian skills you've acquired and can be crucial for resume translation.

Block 10: Physical Profile

Shows your physical readiness profile (e.g., PULHES for Army/Navy/Marine Corps, or AFTMS for Air Force). A profile of "1" or "2" indicates excellent physical condition; higher numbers indicate limitations. This can affect VA disability ratings and retirement eligibility.

Block 11: Separation/Retirement Pay

Indicates any severance pay, lump-sum leave, or retirement pay. If you received severance pay, it may be offset against VA disability compensation (this is called "VA Offset" or "Crackdown").

Block 12: Dependency Status

Shows your dependency status at separation (single, married, with dependents). This affects BAH rates, DEERS enrollment, and TRICARE eligibility for family members.

Block 13: Points Credited for Retirement

For Reserve/National Guard members, this shows total retirement points. You need 50 points per year (with at least one year at 20+ points) for retirement eligibility.

Block 14: Reason for Separation

States why you're leaving: expiration of term of service, completion of initial active duty, medical, convenience of the government, etc. This can affect eligibility for certain benefits.

Block 15: Participation in the Individual Ready Reserve (IRR)

Indicates if you're being transferred to the IRR. IRR members may still be subject to recall in certain circumstances.

Block 16: Period of Active Duty Service

Detailed breakdown of all active duty periods. Essential for VA disability claims to establish when service-connected injuries or illnesses occurred.

Block 17: National Guard or Reserve Service

Shows any Guard or Reserve service periods. Important for calculating retirement points and determining eligibility for Guard/Reserve-specific benefits.

Block 18: Awards Received

Lists specific awards, decorations, and citations. Include combat awards, good conduct medals, and achievement medals.

Block 19: Separation Authority

Identifies the authority under which you're separating (e.g., UCMJ Article 138, AR 635-200, etc.). This determines the legal framework of your separation.

Block 20: Severance Pay Recovery

Indicates if severance pay will be recovered against VA disability compensation. If you received medical separation severance, this block will show the amount and recovery instructions.

Block 21: VA Disability Compensation

Shows any VA disability compensation offsets. If you have a VA rating, this block indicates whether your severance pay is being recovered.

Block 22: Separation Processing

Details the separation processing date and location. Important for tracking when your benefits eligibility begins.

Block 23: Education Benefits

Indicates GI Bill eligibility and remaining entitlement. If you're using the Post-9/11 GI Bill, this block shows your percentage of entitlement and expiration date.

Block 24: Character of Service Determination

Final determination of your character of service. This is the block that most directly affects VA benefits eligibility. An "Honorable" designation is required for most veterans programs.

Block 25: Separation/Retirement Pay

Final pay information, including any lump-sum leave payout and retirement pay calculations.

How to Request a Copy of Your DD-214

If you've lost your DD-214 or need additional copies, you can request them through several channels:

Option 1: National Archives (eVetRecs)

  • Visit vets.archives.gov to submit an online request
  • Processing time: 10-30 business days
  • Free for veterans and next of kin
  • Acceptable for VA claims and benefit applications

Option 2: Through Your Unit Personnel Office

  • Active duty members can request through their unit's S-1 or personnel office
  • Faster processing (often same-day)
  • Best for immediate needs before separation

Option 3: Veterans Service Organization (VSO)

  • Organizations like DAV, VFW, American Legion can assist with requests
  • Often faster than National Archives
  • Free service for veterans

Using Your DD-214 for VA Benefits

Your DD-214 is the foundation of most VA benefit claims. Here's how different sections support specific applications:

VA Disability Compensation

  • Block 4 (Dates of Service): Establishes the period during which your condition must have occurred
  • Block 6 (Discharge Type): Must be honorable or general under honorable conditions
  • Block 16 (Period of Active Duty): Confirms you meet the minimum service requirement (90 days aggregate after Sept. 10, 2001 for Post-9/11 GI Bill; any active duty for disability claims)
  • Block 24 (Character of Service): Critical for establishing eligibility

Post-9/11 GI Bill

  • Block 4: Must show at least 90 days aggregate service after Sept. 10, 2001
  • Block 6: Honorable or general under honorable conditions
  • Block 23: Shows remaining entitlement and expiration date

VA Home Loan Certificate of Eligibility (COE)

  • Block 4: Minimum 90 days active duty for peacetime; 6 years for Guard/Reserve
  • Block 6: Honorable discharge required
  • Block 24: Character of service must be honorable

Common DD-214 Errors and How to Fix Them

Errors on your DD-214 can delay or deny benefits. Watch for these common mistakes:

  • Name spelling errors: Can cause mismatches with SSA and VA records
  • Incorrect dates of service: Affects benefit eligibility calculations
  • Wrong character of service: Can disqualify you from benefits entirely
  • Missing awards/decorations: Loses potential preference points and benefits
  • Incorrect MOS/AFSC: Affects VA disability claims and employment verification

To correct errors: Contact the respective military service's Board for Correction of Military Records (BCMR) within 3 years of separation. Some errors can be corrected even after 3 years if they're administrative mistakes.

DD-214 vs. Other Military Documents

Don't confuse your DD-214 with other military documents:

  • NGB-22 (National Guard): Equivalent DD-214 for Guard members
  • SF-50: Federal civil service document (not military)
  • DD-256/DD-258: Honorable discharge certificates (ceremonial only, not proof of service)
  • Statement of Service: Brief verification letter, not a full DD-214

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a DD-214?

A DD-214 (Certificate of Release or Discharge on Active Duty) is the official military document issued upon separation from active duty. It serves as proof of military service and contains critical information about your character of service, period of service, awards, and eligibility for veterans benefits.

How do I get a copy of my DD-214?

You can request a copy from the National Archives (archives.gov/veterans) or through eVetRecs. Active duty members can also request through their unit personnel office. Processing typically takes 10-30 business days.

Why is my DD-214 important for VA disability?

Your DD-214 is essential for VA disability claims. It proves your service-connected disability occurred during active duty. Field 16 (Period of Service) and Field 24 (Character of Service) are particularly important for establishing eligibility.

What is the difference between DD-214 Member 1 and Member 4?

Member 1 is the copy given to the service member upon separation. Member 4 is retained by the military. Both contain identical information. You may need certified copies for VA claims, employer verification, and other benefits applications.

How long does it take to get a replacement DD-214?

Processing time from the National Archives is typically 10-30 business days. You can expedite by requesting through your local Veterans Service Organization (VSO) or using the eVetRecs online system.

Can I update my DD-214 if there are errors?

Yes. Contact the respective military service's Board for Correction of Military Records (BCMR) within 3 years of separation for most corrections. Some errors can be corrected even after 3 years if they're administrative mistakes.
